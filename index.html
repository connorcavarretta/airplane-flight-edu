Introduction to Flight

For centuries, humans dreamed of soaring through the skies like birds. Today, millions of people fly every day, yet the science behind flight remains fascinating and elegant.

The Science of Flight

Flight isn't magic—it's physics. When you understand how air moves over a wing, how forces balance in midair, and how pilots control their aircraft, the mystery disappears. What remains is something even better: elegant engineering based on fundamental physical laws.

This journey begins with a simple but powerful idea discovered centuries ago: when air speeds up, pressure drops. This is Bernoulli's Principle, and it's the foundation for understanding how wings generate lift.

Bernoulli's Principle

As air speed increases, pressure decreases. This inverse relationship is key to understanding flight.

What You're Seeing

A Venturi tube — a pipe that narrows in the middle then widens. Blue particles show air flowing through:

  • Entrance: Normal speed, normal pressure
  • Throat (narrow): Air speeds up, pressure drops
  • Exit: Air slows down, pressure returns

The Key Insight

Watch the pressure bars below the tube. The middle bar drops because the narrow section forces air to speed up. Faster air = Lower pressure.

Connection to Flight

Wings use this principle! Air flows faster over the curved top than the flat bottom, creating lower pressure above. The pressure difference generates lift.

The Math

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

As velocity (v) increases, pressure (P) must decrease to keep the sum constant.

The Four Forces of Flight

Every aircraft in flight experiences four fundamental forces. Balancing these forces determines how the aircraft moves.

The Four Forces

Lift (green, ↑): Upward force from the wings
Weight (red, ↓): Downward force from gravity
Thrust (blue, →): Forward force from engines
Drag (orange, ←): Backward force from air resistance

Force Balance

Level Flight: Lift = Weight, Thrust = Drag
Climbing: Lift > Weight
Descending: Weight > Lift
Accelerating: Thrust > Drag
Decelerating: Drag > Thrust

What You're Seeing

The airplane in the visualization shows all four forces as colored arrows. Longer arrows = stronger forces. Watch the airplane tilt and move based on which forces are stronger.

The Flight State box shows whether the plane is climbing, descending, accelerating, or maintaining level flight.

Wing Design & Airfoils

The shape of a wing's cross-section (an airfoil) determines how efficiently it generates lift.

What You're Seeing

An airfoil cross-section with flowing streamlines showing how air moves around it. The orange dashed line is the chord line (reference for angle measurement).

Angle of Attack (α)

The angle between the chord line and the oncoming air (relative wind).

Low angle: Less lift
Medium angle (5-10°): Optimal lift
High angle (>15°): STALL - airflow separates, lift drops

Airfoil Types

Cambered: Curved top, generates lift at 0°
Symmetric: Same curve top/bottom
Flat Bottom: Simple design for small planes

Coefficients

CL (Lift Coefficient): How much lift the airfoil generates
CD (Drag Coefficient): How much drag (resistance)
L/D Ratio: Efficiency (higher is better)

  • Increase to 15° → lift maximizes
  • Go above 15° → STALL WARNING appears!
  • Switch airfoil types and see CL/CD change

Control Surfaces

Pilots control aircraft movement using movable surfaces on the wings and tail. Each surface controls rotation around a different axis.

What You're Seeing

A top-down view of an airplane. Orange rectangles are control surfaces — movable parts that deflect airflow to rotate the aircraft.

The Three Axes

Ailerons (Roll): On wing tips, move oppositely. One up, one down = airplane banks left or right.
Elevator (Pitch): On horizontal tail. Deflects up/down to raise or lower the nose.
Rudder (Yaw): On vertical tail. Swings the nose left or right.

How It Works

When you move a control surface, it changes the airflow over that surface, creating more lift on one side. This creates a moment (rotation force) that turns the aircraft around that axis.

Phases of Flight

Every flight follows a sequence of phases, each requiring different configurations and management of the four forces.

What You're Seeing

A side view of an airplane progressing through a complete flight, from takeoff to landing. Watch how the airplane's attitude (nose angle) and altitude change in each phase.

The Five Phases

Takeoff: High thrust, rotate nose up, lift off
Climb: Nose up, gaining altitude
Cruise: Level flight, forces balanced
Descent: Nose down slightly, losing altitude
Landing: Final approach, touchdown

Force Balance

Each phase requires a different relationship between the four forces. The Force Balance indicator shows whether lift equals weight and thrust equals drag for the current phase.

How to Use
